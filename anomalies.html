Misplaced Clicks: Facebook Code in the Article Excerpt

On several websites, the line between content and tracking collapses in amusing ways. Moderators or site editors have inserted Facebook click-tracking scripts directly into the section meant for the article excerpt. Instead of a concise summary of the article, visitors encounter invisible tracking machinery disguised as content.

This error is both literal and metaphorical: the content becomes indistinguishable from the systems that monitor its reception. In the context of this research, it highlights how low-quality production turns news into spectacle where form, function, and interface collide, and attention is captured more for clicks and metrics than for meaning.

Placeholder Chaos: Lorem Ipsum and English in Bulgarian Platforms

Some websites retain placeholder text that was clearly never replaced, leaving English filler where Bulgarian content should appear. Contact forms, article excerpts, and other sections still display generic corporate jargon, revealing that the site was never fully finished or carefully edited.

This anomaly underscores the low-effort, template-driven production of these platforms. Even as they attempt to present themselves as active news sources, their unfinished or mismatched text exposes the performative nature of the content. In line with the spectacle framework, these sites are less about conveying meaningful information and more about creating the appearance of activity and attention is captured through volume and repetition, not through accuracy or polish.

Paid Comments: Foreign-Language Bots on Bulgarian Articles

On some Bulgarian media websites, the comment sections are populated exclusively by bots posting in English or other foreign languages (never in Bulgarian). These automated responses appear under articles written in Bulgarian about domestic topics, creating a striking disconnect between content and engagement.

This pattern strongly suggests the use of paid comment farms operating abroad, rather than genuine audience interaction. Within the spectacle framework, these foreign-language bots emphasize quantity over quality: they simulate activity, create the illusion of discussion, and reinforce the performative nature of the platform, while contributing nothing to meaningful local debate.

A Law That Sees Nothing

Introduced in 2018 (from Delyan Peevski, the media magnate and company), the so-called ‚ÄúPeevski law‚Äù requires media outlets to declare who controls their editorial policy, presenting itself as a tool for transparency. In reality, it relies entirely on self-declaration and offers no mechanism to verify whether the stated control reflects actual influence.

Editorial power rarely operates in explicit or formal ways. It emerges through ownership structures, financial pressure, advertising flows, and synchronized publishing practices, none of which can be captured through a declaration form. Oversight is assigned to the Ministry of Culture, an institution that has openly stated it lacks the expertise and capacity to analyze media control or coordinated behavior.

As a result, the law produces symbolic compliance rather than meaningful accountability. Small independent outlets face real risk from sanctions, while large, structurally coordinated media networks remain formally ‚Äútransparent‚Äù without scrutiny. The anomaly lies not in a failure of enforcement, but in a system designed to simulate regulation while leaving existing power structures intact.
